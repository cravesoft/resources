Global Resource Tracker GitHub

A research tool visualizing 99 non-renewable resource × country pairs spanning fossil fuels, critical minerals, rare earths, industrial metals, and precious metals. Each rectangle's area is proportional to annual production value in USD. Color shows the selected metric — toggle between reserve-to-production ratio, commodity price, resource category, and LLM-scored geopolitical risk. Data sources: EIA International Energy Statistics, USGS Mineral Commodity Summaries 2025, World Bank Pink Sheet, BP Statistical Review 2024.

LLM-powered coloring: The source code includes a full data pipeline and LLM scoring stage. The "Geopolitical Risk" layer rates each resource–country pair on a 0–10 scale: how likely is supply to be disrupted, weaponized, or withheld in ways that harm importing economies? Factors include producer stability, market concentration, availability of substitutes, and historical precedent of export weaponization.

View the Geopolitical Risk scoring prompt
You are an expert analyst in critical minerals, energy security, and geopolitical risk. You will be given a description of a non-renewable natural resource produced in a specific country. Rate the Geopolitical Risk of this resource-country pair on a scale from 0 to 10. Geopolitical Risk measures how likely it is that supply of this resource from this country will be disrupted, weaponized, or withheld in ways that cause serious harm to importing economies. Consider: - The producing country's political stability and governance quality - Historical precedent of supply disruptions or export weaponization - Market concentration (is this country a dominant or irreplaceable supplier?) - Dependence of importing economies (are substitutes or alternative suppliers readily available?) - Alignment with or hostility to major consumer blocs (NATO, EU, US, Japan/Korea) - Strategic importance of the resource to defense, energy, or technology industries Anchors: - 0–1: Minimal risk. Stable democratic producer, multiple alternatives. Examples: iron ore from Australia, gas from Norway. - 2–3: Low risk. Mostly stable producer or moderately concentrated supply with alternatives. Examples: copper from Chile, uranium from Canada. - 4–5: Moderate risk. Significant governance concerns OR concentrated supply, but not both. Examples: uranium from Kazakhstan, nickel from Indonesia. - 6–7: High risk. Dominant supplier with track record of export restrictions, or critical resource from unstable country. Examples: cobalt from DRC, gas from Russia. - 8–9: Very high risk. Near-monopoly of strategically critical resource with demonstrated willingness to weaponize supply. Examples: rare earths from China. - 10: Maximum risk. Complete monopoly of irreplaceable defense-critical resource with active geopolitical confrontation. Respond with ONLY a JSON object: {"exposure": <integer 0-10>, "rationale": "<2-3 sentences>"}

Caveat: Geopolitical risk scores are LLM estimates calibrated to publicly available information as of 2024–2025. They reflect supply-side risk to importers, not investment risk or price volatility. R/P ratios are calculated from proved reserves divided by current production and do not account for future discoveries, demand changes, or technological substitution.
